Drawing Discrimination

Exploring redlining and discriminatory federal New Deal housing policy’s lasting impact on opportunities to build wealth through housing across New York

by RYAN BEST

In the mid-1930’s, there was a widespread assumption that houses in white neighborhoods were much more valuable than those in non-white neighborhoods. The federal government adopted this assumption into federal policy, officially valuing white-owned houses in segregated areas higher than houses in non-white or mixed race neighborhoods.

An excerpt from the 1938 FHA Underwriting Manual – the rulebook for how the federal government determined real estate value at the time.

In the mid to late 1930's, the government also incorporated these discriminatory practices in detailed maps of over 250 American cities that graded each neighborhood on perceived real estate risk; race was one of the main factors within federal policy that determined who was able to get a mortgage and where properties were deemed valuable for decades to come.

D-Graded 'Hazardous' Neighborhoods

Appraisers gave this grading to neighborhoods where they cited:

Very undesirable neighborhood of mixed races ... Italian, Danish, Polish, Swedish, Jewish ... Formerly a good residential district with many well built private homes. Now practically entirely negro with many tenements ... Obsolescence and poor maintenance. Infiltration of Negroes ... Colored infiltration a definitely adverse influence on neighborhood desirability ... Low grade Italian population of questionable occupation and income. Very little likelihood of improvement ...

A-Graded 'Best' Neighborhoods

Appraisers gave this grading to neighborhoods where they cited:

Probably the best area in Staten Island, due to location, homogeneity, and high type of structures .. The major portion of this neighborhood is presently help up by wealthy people, many of whom have large estates overlooking the Hudson. ... A steep bluff separates this area from the yellow and red areas on the east. ... Contains many of the finest and largest private homes. ...

B-Graded 'Still Desirable' and
C-Graded 'Declining' Neighborhoods

Appraisers gave this grading to neighborhoods where they cited:

Homogeneous neighborhood of good houses ... A double carfare has protected this neighborhood from any undesirable infiltration ... A small section of desirable homes well protected from encroachment ... One hour from Times Square by subway, car and bus. Infiltration of lower grade population ... This is rather a mediocre neighborhood with only limited prospects of improvements ... There has been a small settlement of negroes on 99th St. for many years, apparently not spreading. The district may gradually improve ... Mostly developed 30-40 years ago with fairly good grade apartment houses, it has been steadily deteriorating due partly to influx of negroes ...

Studying this map shows us where housing discrimination was strongest in late-1930’s New York. I connected these grades with changes in racial composition and real estate markets from 1940-2010 to better understand where its damaging effects are still felt. Overall, there is a lasting connection between these discriminatory practices, which set the rules for homeownership access and property values for decades to come, and the unequal wealth building opportunities across New York neighborhoods today.

Housing markets are dramatically different in Harlem and the Upper East Side, close neighbors in northern Manhattan. Homeownership was almost non-existent in both neighborhoods in the 1940's, with over 95% of both residents living as tenants. Homeownership has consistently climbed in greenlined Upper East Side in the decades since, now one of the most lucrative real estate markets in the world. In redlined Harlem, often considered the cultural center of black America and one of the only majority non-white neighborhoods in 1940's New York, homeownership has stayed low; homeownership in the Upper East Side is now more than four times greater than in Harlem.

Little Neck and Brookville, two residential neighborhoods in eastern Queens, may show the damaging effects of housing discrimination on property values. Housing prices in these two neighborhoods were a bit closer together before thier racial demographics changed – in 1960, both areas were overwhelmingly white and only $60k separated their average homes (in 2010 dollars). In the decades since, housing prices in now majority-minority Brookville haven't caught up with increases in Little Neck, where the median house is now worth almost $400,000 more.

In the 1940's, greenlined Todt Hill and a redlined area spanning New Brighton, Tompkinsville, and Stapleton were both predominantely white with almost identical property values. In the decades since, Todt Hill has stayed majority white while neighborhoods to its northeast have developed into multi-racial communities. Todt Hill is now one of the more exclusive and expensive neighborhoods in Staten Island, with homeownership rates and property values more than double than the redlined area highlighted.

Brighton Beach has stayed majority white since the 1940's, but its property value increases and homeownership rates haven't outpaced many majority-minority neighborhoods. There is a large immigrant and foreign-born white population in Brighton Beach, especially Russians and Jews, who were also subject to discrimination but are included alongside native born whites in my 'percent white' distinction. The homeownership market in Brighton Beach has remained remarkably consistent with that in gentrifying Crown Heights, which has a substantial Carribbean population.

Even in neighborhoods receiving more favorable redlining grades, the emerging presence of non-white residents was often met with depressed property values and restrained access to homeownership. Fordham hasn't stayed majority white like Country Club, a neighborhood in eastern Bronx. Homeownership rates in Fordham are still dramatically lower than in Country Club, where homes are now almost $200k more valuable.
